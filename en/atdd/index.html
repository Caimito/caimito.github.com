Acceptance Test-Driven Development

How to prevent defects and delight your customers

Acceptance Test-Driven Development is a technique to improve collaboration amongst team members in order to improve product quality. The result will be software better suited for the target purpose and audience. At the same time, the software will be easier to maintain. This reduces your exposure to risk and protects the huge investment your company is making by developing a software product.

Some key success factors

  • Team members and key managers invest into making the product testable
  • Where needed product architecture gets refactored
  • Product Managers participate in creating a specification collaboratively and iteratively
  • Set aside additional time for feature planning
  • Make features as small as possible
  • Create a chain of small features as an on-ramp to a bigger feature

 

ATDD in a nutshell

Acceptance Test-Driven Development follows the scientific method. Development follows these simple, time-tested steps:

Scientists

Software Developers
Define a question What product, feature solves a customer problem?
Gather information and resources Learn as much as you can about the customer problem
Form a hypothesis Define a first set of acceptance criteria as example based scenarios (Given, When, Then)
Test the hypothesis by performing an experient and collecting data in a reproducible manner Create software that matches the acceptance criteria and put it in front of your customers. Gather feedback, performance data, sales numbers, measure impact on your business, etc.
Analyze the data Analyze feedback provided by your customers, runtime performance data, sales numbers, etc.
Interpret the results and draw conclusions that serve as a starting point for a new hypothesis Interpret the results and draw conclusions that serve as a starting point for a new hypothesis
Repeat Repeat

What product, feature solves a customer problem?

Learn as much as you can about the customer problem

Product Management creates meaningful examples of what customers would like to do with the software product. The examples at this stage don't have to follow any formal notation but if people feel comfortable enough, over time they will, they can write them as sentences following the Given/When/Then pattern.

Those examples help the technical team (testers, programmers) to understand that the problem to be solved really is. They also help to convey the customer context to the technical team.

These examples are the starting point for acceptance criteria and tests.
